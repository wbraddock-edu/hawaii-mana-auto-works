Best Tires for Big Island Roads

June 12, 2024 · Mana Auto Works

The Big Island of Hawaii has one of the most diverse road environments in the world. In a single day, you might drive Hilo's perpetually wet streets, the smooth new pavement of Highway 11, the rough lava rock roads near the national park, and the altitude-challenged Saddle Road connecting Hilo to the Kona side. Choosing the right tires for this variety requires more thought than simply buying whatever comes standard on your vehicle.

Wet Performance is Paramount

Hilo is one of the rainiest cities in the United States, receiving over 140 inches of rain per year. This means wet-weather traction isn't just a nice-to-have — it's a safety essential. Look for tires with wide circumferential grooves and advanced tread compounds specifically designed for wet performance. The Michelin Primacy, Bridgestone Turanza, and Continental PureContact are among our top recommendations for Hilo drivers who prioritize wet safety.

Lava Roads Require Durability

If you drive near Kilauea or on lower Puna roads, you'll encounter rough lava surfaces that are essentially sharp volcanic rock. These roads are brutal on tires — we see an elevated rate of sidewall damage and punctures in customers who frequent these areas. For drivers who regularly travel to the park or lower Puna, we recommend tires with reinforced sidewalls and a higher load rating that provides some additional resistance to the jagged rocks.

Our Top Picks for Big Island Drivers

For most Hilo commuters, we recommend the Michelin CrossClimate2 — an all-season tire with exceptional wet performance and good durability. For drivers who regularly head to Saddle Road or Mauna Kea, the Bridgestone Blizzak is worth considering as it handles the cold and occasional ice at elevation while still performing well at lower altitudes. For truck drivers using rough roads, the Toyo Open Country AT III provides the durability and off-road capability these roads demand.

Whatever tires you choose, check pressure weekly — Hawaii's temperature variations between sea level and elevation mean your tire pressure changes significantly throughout the day, which affects handling, fuel economy, and tire wear.
