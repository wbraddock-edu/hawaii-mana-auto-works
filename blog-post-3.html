Lava Road Damage Prevention Guide

June 3, 2024 · Mana Auto Works

The Big Island is the only place in the world with active road construction by volcanic lava flows. While the eruptions at Kilauea are a natural wonder, they create unique hazards for vehicles — from the sharp edges of fresh pahoehoe to the volcanic gas that affects rubber and metal components to the dust created by aa lava fields. Here's what every Big Island driver should know.

Types of Lava Road Hazards

Fresh pahoehoe (smooth, ropy lava) that has recently cooled into road surfaces can have irregular texture and hidden cracks. Aa lava (rough, jagged) used in construction can have extremely sharp edges that will quickly puncture tires if they migrate onto paved surfaces. Volcanic ash and sulfur dioxide gas (vog) from active fissures can damage rubber seals and components with prolonged exposure.

Tire Protection Strategy

If you regularly drive roads near active lava areas, run your tires at the higher end of their recommended pressure range — this improves puncture resistance by keeping the sidewall more rigid against jagged rocks. Carry a tire plug kit and portable inflator — flat tires are significantly more common in lava areas, and the nearest gas station may be 30 miles away. Visually inspect your tires after every drive through lava road areas.

Undercarriage Awareness

Driving over fresh lava fields (never advisable, but sometimes unavoidable on evolving roads) can deposit extremely sharp rock fragments that embed in undercarriage components, fuel lines, and brake lines. After any exposure to fresh lava roads, have your undercarriage professionally inspected. The damage may not be immediately apparent but can cause failures weeks later.

If you must drive near active lava flows, keep windows up and minimize time in areas with significant vog. The sulfur dioxide can accelerate corrosion on electrical components and seals. After exposure, wash your vehicle thoroughly as soon as possible, paying special attention to the undercarriage, wheel wells, and any rubber seals around windows and doors.
