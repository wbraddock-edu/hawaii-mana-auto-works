5 Most Common Car Problems in Hawaii

May 10, 2024 · Mana Auto Works

After 14 years repairing vehicles in Hilo, the team at Mana Auto Works has a clear picture of what fails most often on the Big Island. If you're a new resident or planning to keep a vehicle in Hawaii long-term, knowing these five common issues can help you catch problems early and avoid expensive repairs.

1. Rust and Corrosion

The most common and most severe Hawaii-specific problem. Salt air attacks every unprotected metal surface — brake lines, fuel lines, exhaust systems, and frame components are most vulnerable. We regularly see vehicles that look fine on the outside but have severely compromised undercarriage components. Annual rust inspection and preventive treatment is essential.

2. AC System Failures

Hawaii's perpetual heat means AC systems run constantly and wear out faster. Compressor failures, refrigerant leaks, and condenser damage from road debris are all common. We recommend annual AC performance checks — catching a small refrigerant leak before the compressor runs dry and fails can save $500 or more in repair costs.

3. Tire Damage

Hawaii's road surfaces — from smooth lava pavement to rough rural roads — are harder on tires than mainland asphalt. Sidewall damage from road hazards is significantly more common here. We recommend checking tire pressure weekly and inspecting the sidewalls for bubbles, cuts, or unusual wear patterns monthly.

4. Electrical Issues from Moisture

Humidity and rain find their way into electrical connectors, sensor housings, and control modules, causing corrosion that leads to intermittent failures and check engine lights. We see an elevated rate of sensor failures, particularly O2 sensors, mass airflow sensors, and crankshaft position sensors in vehicles that have been in Hawaii for 5+ years.

5. Premature Rubber Degradation

UV radiation and heat break down rubber faster in Hawaii than in temperate climates. Timing belts, serpentine belts, coolant hoses, power steering hoses, and engine mounts all wear faster here. We recommend inspecting all rubber components at 40,000-mile intervals rather than the 60,000-mile intervals common on the mainland.
